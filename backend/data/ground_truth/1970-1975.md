

# **The Great Transition, 1970-1975**

## **I. Executive Summary**

The period of 1970 to 1975 represents a distinct, disruptive, and definitive inflection point in 20th-century history. This five-year span witnessed the comprehensive unraveling of the political, economic, and social order established in the aftermath of the Second World War. The state of the world was not one of stable continuity but of profound and often painful transition, characterized by a series of cascading shocks that ended the post-war era and established the foundational paradigms of the contemporary world.

Geopolitically, the rigid bipolarity of the Cold War fractured. This was driven by the Nixon administration's "triangular diplomacy," which engineered a stunning diplomatic rapprochement with the People's Republic of China in 1972  and formalized an era of Détente with the Soviet Union, culminating in the Strategic Arms Limitation Treaty (SALT I). This new, complex multipolar dynamic was affirmed by the 1971 entry of the PRC into the United Nations.

Economically, the post-war boom ended decisively. Two sequential shocks capsized the global system. First, the 1971 "Nixon Shock" unilaterally destroyed the Bretton Woods system, ending the gold standard and ushering in an era of volatile floating currencies. Second, the 1973 OPEC oil embargo, launched in the wake of the Yom Kippur War, quadrupled crude oil prices and weaponized energy. This toxic combination created "stagflation", a novel and debilitating condition of high inflation and high unemployment that plunged the industrial world into the 1973-1975 recession.

This economic turmoil was mirrored by a deep crisis of confidence in Western institutions. In the United States, the Watergate scandal (1972-1974) consumed the presidency, leading to the first-ever resignation of a US president and a profound public disillusionment with the government. This political paralysis was compounded by the final, humiliating collapse of South Vietnam in the 1975 Fall of Saigon, marking a definitive end to American military intervention.

Amid this crisis, powerful social and technological transformations were underway. Second-wave feminism moved from protest to policy, achieving landmark victories such as *Roe v. Wade* and Title IX. The environmental movement, mobilized by the 1970 Earth Day, forced the creation of foundational legal protections like the US Environmental Protection Agency. Technologically, while the Apollo-Soyuz mission in 1975 symbolized the end of the Space Race, the 1971 invention of the Intel 4004 microprocessor quietly initiated the digital revolution.

Conclusively, the state of the world in 1975 was one of fundamental disruption. The certainties of the post-war system were gone, replaced by a far more complex, fragmented, and volatile landscape of floating currencies, multipolar geopolitics, and new, media-driven global threats.

## **II. Global Overview**

### **Geopolitical Landscape: The Fracturing of the Bipolar World**

The 1970-1975 period was defined by a strategic realignment that replaced the rigid US-Soviet bipolarity with a more fluid and complex "triangular" power dynamic. This shift was not born of idealism but of mutual strategic necessity. The United States, bogged down in Vietnam and facing domestic turmoil, sought to reduce tensions. The Soviet Union, facing severe economic stagnation and a hostile split with China, also saw benefits in accommodation.

This "Détente" produced the 1972 Strategic Arms Limitation Treaty (SALT I), the first agreement to place quantitative limits on the two superpowers' nuclear arsenals, including a treaty limiting Anti-Ballistic Missile (ABM) systems. SALT I was considered the crowning achievement of the Nixon-Kissinger strategy.

The true catalyst for this new landscape, however, was the "week that changed the world": President Nixon's 1972 visit to the People's Republic of China. This seismic event formally ended 23 years of diplomatic isolation. Its strategic genius was in its exploitation of the deep Sino-Soviet split. By opening relations with Beijing, Washington gained powerful leverage over Moscow, which now feared a two-front strategic encirclement. The Soviets, who had previously stalled on arms control, quickly "changed their tune," leading directly to the successful Moscow summit and the signing of SALT I just months after Nixon's return from Beijing.

This rapprochement was made possible by a critical 1971 development: the passage of UN General Assembly Resolution 2758\. This resolution recognized the People's Republic of China as "the only legitimate representative of China to the United Nations," expelling the representatives of the Republic of China (Taiwan). This action conferred immense international legitimacy on the PRC and granted it a permanent, veto-wielding seat on the Security Council, cementing its status as a third global superpower.

The apex of Détente was the 1975 Conference on Security and Co-operation in Europe, which produced the Helsinki Accords. Signed by 35 nations, including the US and USSR, this agreement represented a monumental, if paradoxical, bargain. The Soviet Union achieved its primary foreign policy goal in "Basket 1": the formal, international recognition of the post-WWII borders in Europe, which effectively legitimized Soviet control over Eastern Europe. In exchange, the West secured "Basket 3," a series of commitments on "respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms, including the freedom of thought, conscience, religion or belief". The Soviet leadership, confident in its internal controls, viewed this as a minor, non-binding concession. This assumption was a historic miscalculation. The human rights provisions of Basket 3 were immediately seized upon by dissident movements within the Eastern Bloc, leading to the creation of monitoring groups like Helsinki Watch. These groups used the Accords as an internationally recognized standard to "name and shame" the Soviet Union for its own human rights violations, helping to galvanize the internal opposition that would, over the following decade, contribute to the erosion and eventual collapse of Soviet legitimacy.

### **Global Economy: The Great Disruption**

The 1970-1975 period marked the definitive, violent end of the post-war economic order, an era known in France as the *Trente Glorieuses* or "Thirty Glorious Years." This collapse was triggered by two distinct but interconnected shocks.

The first was the "Nixon Shock" of August 15, 1971\. Facing rising inflation and dwindling gold reserves—drained by the deficits of the Vietnam War and "Great Society" social spending —President Nixon unilaterally and abruptly suspended the convertibility of the US dollar to gold. This single act destroyed the "linchpin" of the Bretton Woods system, which had governed the global economy since 1944 on a system of fixed exchange rates pegged to a gold-backed dollar. The immediate result was the 1971 Smithsonian Agreement, a temporary fix that devalued the dollar, but by 1973 the system of fixed rates was abandoned entirely. This ushered in the modern era of floating currencies, unleashing a new and persistent volatility on global markets.

The second shock was the 1973 Oil Crisis. On October 19, 1973, in response to US support for Israel during the Yom Kippur War, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC) declared a total oil embargo against the United States and other pro-Israel nations. OAPEC also initiated production cuts that caused the world price of oil to quadruple in just a few months, from approximately $2.90 a barrel to $11.65 a barrel. This acted as a massive supply shock to an industrial world built on the foundation of cheap, abundant energy.

These two shocks were deeply linked. The standard narrative of the oil crisis is purely political. However, the economic tinder was already present. Oil was priced in US dollars. When the Nixon Shock of 1971 de-linked the dollar from gold, the dollar's value began to fall, meaning OAPEC nations were being paid in a depreciating asset. Their real income was plummeting. The oil producers, therefore, already had a powerful *economic* incentive to raise prices dramatically to "re-peg" the value of their oil to a more stable asset, like gold. The Yom Kippur War provided the perfect *political pretext* and unified front to execute a price hike that was already economically necessary for their interests.

The combined result of the currency collapse and the energy shock was "stagflation". This was a new, toxic economic phenomenon: the simultaneous existence of high inflation (driven by energy costs) and high unemployment (driven by slow growth). This condition defied the established Keynesian economic models (the Phillips Curve), which held that inflation and unemployment had an inverse relationship. Policymakers were left without a coherent response. The 1973–1975 recession that followed was the most severe global economic contraction since the Great Depression, marking a painful end to a generation of prosperity.

### **Social Trends: A World in Flux**

The era's economic and political crises were set against a backdrop of profound social and cultural change, as movements born in the 1960s matured into powerful institutional forces.

Second-wave feminism, in particular, moved from protest to policy, achieving monumental, concrete victories. In the United States, Title IX of the Federal Education Amendments (1972) made discrimination based on sex illegal in federally funded education programs, fundamentally reshaping academics and athletics. The following year, the 1973 Supreme Court decision in *Roe v. Wade* legalized abortion nationwide, a landmark victory for the movement's focus on bodily autonomy and reproductive freedom. In 1974, the Equal Credit Opportunity Act prohibited creditors from discriminating based on sex or marital status, giving women independent economic power for the first time.

The environmental movement exploded into the public consciousness as a mainstream political force. The first Earth Day on April 22, 1970, was a watershed event, mobilizing an estimated 20 million Americans in a "national teach-in" to demand action on pollution. This massive grassroots demonstration had a direct and immediate impact: by December 1970, it had provided the political mandate for the US Congress to authorize the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to enforce new environmental laws.

Other liberation movements also achieved critical victories. The Gay Liberation movement, which had become a public force after 1969, won a crucial battle for destigmatization in 1973 when the American Psychiatric Association, after intense lobbying and protest, voted to remove homosexuality from its list of psychiatric disorders. The anti-Vietnam War movement reached its apogee in 1970 following the US invasion of Cambodia. The invasion sparked massive student strikes across over 650 campuses. The movement was galvanized by the tragedy at Kent State University in Ohio, where National Guard troops killed four unarmed student protestors, an event that horrified the nation and deepened the divides in American society.

### **Environmental State**

The period of 1970-1975 was arguably the most foundational in the history of modern environmental law. The public activism of Earth Day 1970 was rapidly converted into a powerful legal and regulatory framework. The creation of the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in December 1970 provided the mechanism for enforcement. This was followed by a wave of landmark legislation passed with rare bipartisan support, including the National Environmental Education Act (1970), the Occupational Safety and Health Act (OSHA) (1970), the Clean Air Act (1970), and the Clean Water Act (1972).

The movement also became transnational and more confrontational with the founding of Greenpeace in Canada in 1971\. Born from protests against nuclear testing, Greenpeace pioneered a new model of "direct action," using media-savvy stunts to "bear witness" to and expose environmental problems like commercial whaling. This new brand of activism demonstrated the growing global reach of environmental concerns.

### **Global Flashpoints**

While superpower Détente lowered the global temperature, regional "hot wars"—many of them proxies for the Cold War—remained critical flashpoints.

1. **The Middle East:** Following the 1973 Yom Kippur War, the region remained the world's most volatile flashpoint. The war ended in a UN-brokered ceasefire, not a permanent peace. The conflict had now inextricably linked regional stability to the global economy through the proven "oil weapon".  
2. **Southeast Asia:** The 1973 Paris Peace Accords were a "cease-fire in place" that left hundreds of thousands of North Vietnamese soldiers in South Vietnam. This arrangement was inherently unstable, guaranteeing a future conflict that erupted with the North's final offensive in 1975\.  
3. **Angola:** The 1974 Carnation Revolution in Portugal led to a rapid and chaotic decolonization. The resulting power vacuum in Angola in 1975 was immediately filled by rival nationalist groups. This conflict swiftly became the new premier proxy battleground for the Cold War, with the Soviet Union and Cuba supporting the MPLA, while the United States and South Africa backed the rival FNLA and UNITA factions.  
4. **Cyprus:** A 1974 coup d'état, sponsored by the ruling military junta in Greece, was followed by a full-scale military invasion by Turkey. The conflict resulted in the permanent division of the island and created a severe, lasting rift *within* the NATO alliance, critically weakening its southern flank.

### **Emerging Paradigms**

Beyond the headlines of war and recession, three nascent developments emerged that would have disruptive, long-term global implications.

1. **The Microprocessor:** In 1971, Intel, a young Silicon Valley company, released the 4004\. Developed for a Japanese calculator company, it was the world's first "programmable logic microchip". This was a true paradigm shift. Previous computing logic was "hard-wired" into circuits for specific tasks. The 4004 put a general-purpose Central Processing Unit (CPU) onto a single, mass-producible chip; the logic was now in the *software*. This "democratization of the computer" was the technical and conceptual "Big Bang" that ignited the personal computing revolution.  
2. **Genetic Engineering (and its Oversight):** As the science of creating "recombinant DNA" (joining DNA from different organisms) became a reality, the scientific community faced an unknown biohazard. In a historically unprecedented move, scientists, led by Paul Berg, called for a voluntary moratorium on their own research. This led to the 1975 Asilomar Conference. At this meeting, scientists, lawyers, and ethicists publicly debated the risks and established the world's first precautionary guidelines for biotechnology. This established a new and enduring model of scientific self-regulation.  
3. **Transnational Terrorism:** Terrorism evolved from a localized tactic to a global, media-driven strategy. This was horrifically demonstrated at the 1972 Munich Olympics. The Palestinian group "Black September" took the Israeli team hostage, and the subsequent, botched rescue attempt was broadcast live to a global audience. The terrorists, though all killed or captured, achieved their primary objective: broadcasting their cause to billions. Munich proved that media spectacle was a more powerful force multiplier than the attack itself, creating the blueprint for modern international terrorism.

### **Systemic Fragilities**

The events of 1970-1975 did not just cause temporary crises; they revealed permanent, systemic fragilities in the global order.

1. **Energy Dependence:** The 1973 crisis exposed the fact that the entire industrial world's economy was fundamentally fragile, built on a single-point-of-failure supply chain: cheap, abundant oil from the Persian Gulf. This vulnerability, previously theoretical, was now a proven and exploitable reality.  
2. **The Floating Currency System:** The post-1971 world of floating exchange rates was a massive, untested experiment. The resulting currency volatility  and instability created a new, systemic fragility in global finance, a problem that economists noted would persist.  
3. **Erosion of Western Institutional Trust:** In the West's hegemonic power, the United States, the combination of a long, divisive war in Vietnam and the deep-seated criminality of the Watergate scandal created a systemic breakdown of public faith in government, the presidency, and established institutions.

## **III. Major Powers and Regional Blocs**

### **The United States**

Domestic Politics  
The period 1970-1975 was arguably the most tumultuous in American domestic political history since the Civil War. The nation was consumed by the Watergate scandal, a political crisis that began with a "third-rate burglary attempt" at the Democratic National Committee headquarters in June 1972\. Despite being re-elected in a 49-state landslide in 1972 72, President Richard Nixon's administration unraveled as investigative journalism and televised Senate hearings in 1973, led by Senator Sam Ervin, revealed a vast, criminal conspiracy of political espionage, sabotage, and obstruction of justice. The crisis culminated in the "smoking gun" tape, which proved the president's complicity from the beginning.14 Facing certain impeachment, Nixon became the first and only US president to resign, on August 9, 1974\.  
His successor, Gerald Ford, became the first person to hold the office without being elected president or vice president. Ford's presidency was immediately defined by his controversial September 1974 pardon of Nixon, which he argued was necessary to heal the nation but which a deeply cynical public largely saw as a corrupt bargain. This act cemented a profound and lasting popular disillusionment with the government. Ford's administration then struggled to address the deepening economic crisis, with his "Whip Inflation Now" (WIN) public relations campaign seen as a simplistic and ineffective response to the severe stagflation crippling the nation.

Foreign Policy and International Relations  
This domestic chaos stood in stark contrast to the administration's ambitious and transformative foreign policy, engineered by Nixon and his National Security Advisor (later Secretary of State) Henry Kissinger. The primary goal in Vietnam was to secure "Peace with Honor". This led to the 1970 invasion of Cambodia to pressure Hanoi and, ultimately, the signing of the Paris Peace Accords on January 27, 1973\. The accords allowed for the withdrawal of US troops and the return of American prisoners of war. However, the agreement was fatally flawed, as it allowed North Vietnamese forces to remain in the South. When North Vietnam launched its final, conventional offensive in 1975, the US, paralyzed by Watergate and with Congress unwilling to provide the aid Nixon had secretly promised, was unable to respond. The war ended in a chaotic and humiliating evacuation, with the Fall of Saigon on April 30, 1975\.  
While the Vietnam War ended in failure, the Nixon-Kissinger doctrine of "triangular diplomacy" fundamentally re-engineered the Cold War through the 1972 rapprochement with China  and the 1972 SALT I treaty with the Soviet Union.

Economic Performance  
The US economy was in deep distress. The "Nixon Shock" of 1971, which included domestic wage-and-price controls and the international suspension of gold convertibility , failed to stop inflation. The 1973 oil shock acted as an accelerant, plunging the nation into the 1973-1975 recession. This period of stagflation was marked by high unemployment, crippling inflation, lengthening gas lines, and a decline in real wages and living standards.  
Technological Advancements  
The era marked a pivot in the US space program. The Apollo moon program, a symbol of 1960s triumphalism, ended with the Apollo 17 mission in December 1972.80 The national focus shifted to long-duration space flight with the Skylab space station, which hosted three crews from 1973-1974 82, and to international cooperation, symbolized by the 1975 Apollo-Soyuz Test Project with the Soviet Union.

### **The Soviet Union (USSR)**

Domestic Politics  
This period represented the zenith of the Brezhnev era, an era officially lauded as "Developed Socialism" but retrospectively defined as the "Era of Stagnation". General Secretary Leonid Brezhnev's core domestic policy was a rejection of Khrushchev-era reforms in favor of conservative stability. He guaranteed the Communist Party apparatus, or nomenklatura, its most cherished privilege: "stability of cadres," or an end to term limits and political purges. This stability was enforced by the omnipresent KGB, led by Yuri Andropov, which systematically suppressed the growing dissident movement. Brezhnev's nationality policy also quietly re-asserted Russian dominance, notably by purging the leadership of the Ukrainian Communist Party in 1972-73 to crush its assertions of cultural autonomy.  
Foreign Policy and International Relations  
Brezhnev pursued a pragmatic, dual-track foreign policy. On one track, he championed Détente with the West. This was not for ideological reasons but to achieve key strategic goals: slowing the economically ruinous arms race (SALT I, 1972\) , gaining access to Western technology and grain, and, most critically, securing formal recognition of the USSR's post-WWII empire in Eastern Europe, which he achieved at the Helsinki Accords (1975).  
On the second track, the USSR aggressively exploited Western weakness and post-colonial opportunities to expand its own influence. It armed and supported the Egyptian and Syrian attack on Israel in 1973, backed North Vietnam's final victory in 1975, and intervened decisively with Cuban proxy forces to install a Marxist government in the newly independent Angola in 1975\.

Economic Performance  
The Soviet command economy was a house of cards, propped up by a global crisis. Structurally, the system was failing; its collective agricultural sector was a disaster, unable to feed its own people and necessitating massive annual grain imports from the West, including the United States. However, the 1973 oil price spike provided a massive, unearned windfall. As a major oil and gas producer, the USSR was suddenly flush with hard currency, which Brezhnev used to purchase Western grain and technology. This influx of "petrodollars" masked the deep, structural stagnation and postponed the inevitable economic reckoning, while living standards for the average citizen began to stagnate and decline.  
Technological Advancements  
The Soviet space program focused on mastering long-duration space flight, launching the Salyut series of space stations. This work provided the foundation for their participation in the 1975 Apollo-Soyuz Test Project, a powerful symbol of Détente's cooperative potential.

### **The People's Republic of China (PRC)**

Domestic Politics  
The PRC was internally consumed by the chaotic, final years of the Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution. The period began with a profound succession crisis. In 1971, Lin Biao, Mao's designated successor and military chief, died in a mysterious plane crash after allegedly attempting a coup. This event created a political vacuum and discredited the radical phase of the Cultural Revolution. In the ensuing power struggle, a radical faction, later known as the "Gang of Four" and led by Mao's wife Jiang Qing, battled for control against a pragmatic faction led by Premier Zhou Enlai.91 This internal struggle led to the cautious rehabilitation of Deng Xiaoping in 1973, who had been purged years earlier but was brought back by Zhou to help stabilize the state and economy.  
Foreign Policy and International Relations  
Driven by a deep and abiding fear of the Soviet Union following the 1969 Sino-Soviet border clashes , China executed a stunning strategic pivot, emerging from two decades of self-imposed isolation. This began with "Ping-Pong Diplomacy" in 1971, a soft diplomatic overture using a visit by the US table-tennis team. This was followed by two monumental victories: the 1971 admission to the UN, which gave the PRC the sole China seat , and the 1972 Nixon visit. This historic meeting formally realigned global politics, establishing the "strategic triangle" (US-USSR-PRC) and marking China's arrival as a central player on the world stage.  
Economic Performance  
The economy was still recovering from the massive dislocations of the Cultural Revolution. Zhou Enlai, and later Deng Xiaoping, attempted to push for pragmatic modernization and restore economic production, often clashing with the "Gang of Four," who insisted on ideological purity over economic efficiency.

### **European Economic Community (EEC)**

Domestic Politics and Expansion  
The EEC cemented its status as a formidable global economic bloc through its first and most significant enlargement. On January 1, 1973, the EEC expanded from its six founding members to nine, admitting the United Kingdom, Ireland, and Denmark. This historic expansion, which had been vetoed for years by French President Charles de Gaulle, was only possible after his resignation. The UK's membership, championed by Prime Minister Edward Heath, was politically contentious. His successor, Harold Wilson, held a national referendum in 1975 on whether to remain in the EEC. The British public voted "Yes" by a decisive 67.2% margin, confirming the UK's place in Europe.  
Foreign Policy and International Relations  
The EEC began to develop its own cohesive policies, such as the "polluter pays" environmental principle. More significantly, events on its periphery signaled a democratic future. The 1974 Carnation Revolution in Portugal and the death of fascist dictator Francisco Franco in Spain in 1975 ended decades of authoritarian rule, paving the way for both nations to democratize and eventually join the EEC.  
Economic Performance  
The newly enlarged Community was immediately thrust into crisis. It was heavily impacted by the 1973 oil crisis and the ensuing global recession. This economic strain forced the bloc to create new cooperative mechanisms to manage growing economic disparities between its members, such as the European Regional Development Fund (1974).

### **Japan**

Domestic Politics  
Japan's political scene remained remarkably stable under the "1955 System," which was characterized by the continuous, dominant rule of the Liberal Democratic Party (LDP). This political stability provided the backdrop for a period of extreme economic drama.  
Economic Performance  
The years 1970-1972 marked the final, frenzied peak of Japan's post-war "economic miracle". The 1973 Oil Crisis, known in Japan as the "Oil Shokku", was a national trauma. As a nation almost entirely dependent on imported energy, Japan was the most vulnerable of all industrial powers. The crisis triggered widespread public panic, including hoarding of toilet paper and detergents  and in 1974, it led to Japan's first-ever year of negative GDP growth in the post-war era, a contraction of 2%.  
Economic Adaptation  
Japan's response to this crisis is the key story. Instead of collapsing, the nation executed a rapid and profoundly successful strategic pivot. The "Shokku" forced the hard, structural changes that other Western nations, like the US, delayed. Government and industry, led by the powerful Ministry of International Trade and Industry (MITI), embarked on a national crusade to cut energy consumption and shift production away from heavy, energy-intensive industries. Japan became the world leader in energy efficiency, conservation, and the development of high-technology, high-value-added sectors such as consumer electronics and fuel-efficient automobiles. The 1974 recession, while painful, was the necessary price for this strategic transformation. This pivot built the industrial base that would allow Japan to achieve spectacular economic dominance in the 1980s.

## **IV. Thematic Sections**

### **Science and Technology: Seeds of the Future**

This period witnessed the end of one great technological era and the quiet, simultaneous birth of two others.

The "Moon Race," a defining feature of the Cold War, officially concluded. The United States flew its final lunar landing, Apollo 17, in December 1972\. The focus of human spaceflight immediately pivoted from exploration to *habitation* and *cooperation*. The US launched Skylab, its first space station, in 1973\. Three successive crews inhabited the station from 1973-1974, with the final mission lasting a record 84 days. This program provided crucial data proving that humans could live and work productively in space for extended periods, laying the scientific foundation for all future space stations. This new era of cooperation was powerfully symbolized by the 1975 Apollo-Soyuz Test Project. A US Apollo capsule and a Soviet Soyuz module docked in orbit. The globally televised "handshake in space" between commanders Thomas Stafford and Aleksey Leonov marked the definitive, friendly end of the Space Race and served as a potent symbol of Détente.

While spaceflight dominated headlines, the most impactful technological event of the era was scarcely noticed. In 1971, Intel released the 4004 microprocessor. It was the first "programmable logic microchip," a complete CPU on a single chip. This was a revolutionary conceptual leap. It shifted the "brain" of a machine from its fixed, permanent *hardware* to its flexible, adaptable *software*. This "democratization of the computer" was the technical foundation for the entire personal computer revolution and the digital age that would follow.

A third revolution began in biology. As scientists developed the ability to "cut and paste" DNA from different organisms—a technique called recombinant DNA—the scientific community itself raised alarms. In an unprecedented act of self-regulation, leading researchers called for a voluntary pause on experiments. This led to the 1975 Asilomar Conference in California, where scientists, lawyers, and ethicists gathered to publicly debate the potential biohazards. The conference successfully produced the first-ever guidelines for biotechnology, establishing a model of scientific and ethical oversight that has influenced the field ever since.

### **Global Health: A Unifying Victory**

The 1970-1975 period was one of the brightest in the history of public health, as it marked the critical endgame for the global eradication of smallpox. This campaign stands as one of the 20th century's few unqualified triumphs of global cooperation.

At the end of the 1960s, smallpox was still endemic in large parts of Africa and Asia. The World Health Organization (WHO) Smallpox Eradication Programme (SEP), which began in 1966, had by this point shifted its strategy. Earlier, inefficient efforts at mass vaccination were replaced by a highly effective, two-pronged "surveillance and containment" approach. First, dedicated surveillance teams, often on motorcycles, actively hunted for cases in remote villages and markets, showing pictures to identify potential outbreaks. Second, upon confirmation of a case, teams would immediately descend and perform "focal vaccination," creating a "ring" of immunity around the infected household and its contacts to break the chain of transmission.

This strategy was incredibly successful. The number of endemic countries dropped rapidly. This victory was not just medical; it was geopolitical. The SEP was one of the few, rare areas of active, constructive collaboration between the United States and the Soviet Union. Both superpowers jointly funded the WHO program, provided vaccines, and shared intelligence, viewing the eradication of a common human disease as a goal that transcended Cold War rivalry. This effort, a tangible success of Détente, led to the last naturally occurring case in 1977 and the formal declaration of eradication in 1980\.

### **Security and Conflict: The Changing Face of War**

This period saw the full spectrum of modern conflict: conventional warfare, the end of a proxy war, the beginning of a genocide, and the rise of a new form of transnational terrorism.

The 1973 Yom Kippur War was a textbook conventional conflict. A coalition led by Egypt and Syria launched a devastating surprise attack on Israel during the Jewish holy day of Yom Kippur.8 While Israel's military, after initial, catastrophic losses, was able to reorganize and win a tactical victory, the war was a strategic victory for the Arab coalition. It shattered the myth of Israeli invincibility that had held since 1967\. The war had three major consequences: it triggered a superpower crisis that brought the US and USSR to the brink of nuclear confrontation, it unleashed the OPEC "oil weapon" , and its psychological shock paved the way for the 1978 Camp David Accords and the first Arab-Israeli peace treaty.

The Vietnam War, the quintessential Cold War proxy conflict, came to its definitive end. The 1973 Paris Peace Accords were merely an intermission. In early 1975, North Vietnam launched a full-scale *conventional* military offensive. The South Vietnamese army, without the promised US air support—which the Watergate-paralyzed US Congress refused to authorize 17—collapsed completely. The war concluded on April 30, 1975, with the capture of the Presidential Palace in Saigon.

Just two weeks earlier, another war in Southeast Asia had ended. The Cambodian Civil War concluded on April 17, 1975, when the Maoist Khmer Rouge, led by Pol Pot, captured the capital of Phnom Penh. This marked the beginning of "Year Zero" and one of the 20th century's most brutal genocides.

Finally, this period saw the birth of modern, media-driven terrorism. On September 5, 1972, during the Munich Olympics, the Palestinian group "Black September" attacked the Olympic Village, killing two Israeli athletes and taking nine hostage. A botched German rescue attempt was broadcast live to a global audience of nearly a billion people, ending in the deaths of all the hostages, a policeman, and five terrorists. The terrorists, despite their operational failure, had "won." They demonstrated a new paradigm: the goal was not military victory but the capture of the global media, using the spectacle of violence to broadcast a political message.

### **Culture and Society: Fragmentation and Liberation**

The collective, often utopian, ethos of the 1960s counter-culture faded, replaced by a more fragmented and individualistic landscape. The 1970 breakup of The Beatles was a symbolic marker of this shift. The monolithic "youth culture" splintered into myriad subcultures, each with its own sound and style, from the glitter of Glam Rock and the escapism of Disco to the raw, nihilistic energy of Punk Rock. In cinema, the "New Hollywood" movement produced some of the era's most iconic and critical films, such as *The Godfather* (1972).

The most significant social movement of the era was feminism, which had profound cultural and legal triumphs. The 1973 "Battle of the Sexes" tennis match, where Billie Jean King defeated self-proclaimed male chauvinist Bobby Riggs, was a globally televised cultural event that served as a powerful metaphor for the broader women's rights movement. This cultural victory was backed by substantive legal change, most notably the 1973 *Roe v. Wade* Supreme Court ruling and the 1972 passage of Title IX. The movement also diversified, recognizing that the "liberation" of white, middle-class women was not the same as that of others. This led to the founding of the National Black Feminist Organization in 1973 and the 1973 adoption of a resolution by the National Organization for Women (NOW) recognizing lesbian rights.

This period also saw the rise of a new and influential global actor: the international Non-Governmental Organization (NGO). Greenpeace (founded 1971\) pioneered non-violent "direct action" protests to expose environmental issues. Amnesty International (founded 1961\) grew dramatically in the 1970s, widening its mission from "prisoners of conscience" to include a global campaign against torture. Its "letter-writing" campaigns proved remarkably effective, demonstrating that a coordinated, transnational "conscience" could successfully apply moral pressure to sovereign governments and secure the release of political prisoners.

## **V. Conclusion**

The period of 1970 to 1975 represents a five-year "state of shock" during which the fundamental systems governing the post-war world collapsed. The key findings of this report show a world in the throes of a chaotic and painful transition, where every pillar of the old order—political, economic, military, and social—was shaken.

This was, definitively, an era of endings. The post-war economic boom, built on the 1944 Bretton Woods agreement and cheap energy, ended in 1971 and 1973\. It was replaced by the volatile, untested system of floating currencies and the harsh new reality of "stagflation". The post-war geopolitical structure of rigid bipolarity was deliberately fractured, replaced by a complex triangular game. The post-war American "imperial presidency" ended in the disgrace of Watergate, and the post-war US mission to contain communism by force ended in the humiliating Fall of Saigon. Even the triumphant "Space Race" that defined the 1960s came to a symbolic close with the cooperative handshake of Apollo-Soyuz.

Yet, for every system that ended, a new one began. This period was an era of disruptive origins. The collapse of Bretton Woods gave birth to the modern system of global finance. The 1973 oil crisis gave birth to the energy-conscious, high-technology economy, pioneered by Japan. The 1971 admission of the PRC to the UN  and the 1972 Nixon visit  marked the birth of our modern multipolar world.

The long-term implications are profound. The technological "DNA" of the 21st century was set in this period: the 1971 invention of the microprocessor was the discrete event that began the digital revolution, while the 1975 Asilomar conference established the ethical framework for the biotechnology era. The 1972 Munich Massacre provided the blueprint for media-driven transnational terrorism.

The state of the world in 1975 was far more complex, unstable, and fragmented than it had been in 1970\. It was a world gripped by economic pessimism, institutional disillusionment, and new, intractable forms of conflict. However, in that chaotic rupture, the political, economic, and technological paradigms that define the 21st century were definitively established.
