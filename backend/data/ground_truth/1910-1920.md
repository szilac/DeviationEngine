

# **The Decade of Rupture: A Global Analysis, 1911–1920**

## **I. Executive Summary**

The decade spanning 1911 to 1920 constitutes a catastrophic global rupture, defined primarily by the terminal crisis of European imperial competition—the Great War (1914–1918)—and its destabilizing, revolutionary aftermath. This period violently dismantled the 19th-century world order, replacing the relatively stable European multipolar system with an intensely volatile structure characterized by profound ideological schism and a radical shift in financial power.

**Major Political Shifts:** The period opened with a fragile balance maintained by secret, interlocking imperial alliances (Triple Entente versus Central Powers) that had transitioned to an offensive posture, heightening war tension and leading directly to the 1914 conflagration.1 By 1920, four major empires—the Russian, Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and German—had formally collapsed, giving rise to numerous, often unstable, successor states.3 The subsequent peace was formalized by the punitive Treaty of Versailles, which mandated crippling reparations and fostered deep German resentment.5 Although the global community attempted to establish the League of Nations as the first forum for collective security 6, the institution was critically compromised from its inception by the United States’ failure to ratify the treaty and join its own creation, hamstringing its power to mediate or enforce global stability.7

**Economic Changes:** The stability provided by the Gold Standard was shattered in 1914 as most nations suspended convertibility, leading to soaring inflation rates globally.9 The massive debt accrued by European belligerents resulted in the loss of over a third of their total external investment accumulated during the 19th century.10 The singular most important economic realignment was the instantaneous transition of the United States from a net global debtor to the world's dominant creditor by 1919\.10 This marked the definitive beginning of the end for the City of London’s century-long financial hegemony and signaled the ascendancy of Wall Street.10

**Social Developments:** Wartime mobilization acted as a catalyst for massive social restructuring. The expanded employment of women in industrial and auxiliary roles accelerated the long-running campaign for political equality, culminating in major suffrage victories in key nations, notably the ratification of the Nineteenth Amendment in the U.S. in 1920\.12 However, the period was also marked by deep socioeconomic tension, exemplified by the widespread global labor unrest, including the extensive U.S. strike wave of 1919 14, and the successful consolidation of the radically new ideological power of Bolshevik Communism in Russia.15

**Impactful Crises:** The Great War was compounded by the devastating 1918–1919 Influenza Pandemic. This health crisis caused a demographic shock far exceeding the military casualties, killing an estimated 50–100 million people globally 16 and exposing profound vulnerabilities in public health infrastructure and societal resilience.

**Scientific and Technological Advancements:** Military necessity dramatically matured several key technologies, including aviation (transitioning rapidly from reconnaissance to operational striking power) 18 and battlefield medicine, particularly through the refinement and widespread implementation of safe blood transfusion techniques.19 Intellectually, the verification of Albert Einstein's General Theory of Relativity in 1919, which fundamentally redefined gravity and the structure of the cosmos, overthrew 200 years of Newtonian physics and inaugurated modern theoretical cosmology.20

**Overall "State of the World":** The world concluded 1920 deeply fractured, financially exhausted, and politically unstable. The peace settlements failed to resolve the underlying issues, instead replacing old imperial tensions with new, intense nationalistic, ideological, and economic grievances. Stability was replaced by volatility, ensuring the next decade would be defined by ongoing systemic stresses and revisionist political movements.

## **II. Global Overview: The Decade of Rupture**

### **A. Geopolitical Landscape**

The European geopolitical architecture entered the decade rigidly polarized. The alliance system, encompassing the Triple Entente and the Central Powers, had evolved beyond purely defensive guarantees into a heightened war footing. By 1910, these alliances were perceived as structures for offense, increasing war tension and fueling the arms race. Germany, feeling "encircled," adopted increasingly aggressive foreign policies, while imperial rivalries among Britain, France, Russia, and Germany intensified the distrust that formed the Triple Entente.2

This systemic fragility was repeatedly tested by regional crises. The Agadir crisis in Morocco (1911) demonstrated the willingness of powers to push confrontation. More consequentially, the Balkan Wars of 1912–1913 resulted in huge casualties and drastically altered the regional balance of power. The initial conflict stripped the Ottoman Empire of nearly all its remaining European territory, including Macedonia and Albania. The resulting rise of Serbian and Russian power deeply concerned Austria-Hungary, confirming that a small dispute in the region could rapidly escalate into a major conflict.

The post-war settlement attempted to reshape global governance, but the mechanism of peace contained intrinsic flaws. The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed immense burdens on Germany, including military crippling, territorial losses (such as the Polish Corridor), and reparations totaling 20 billion gold marks, which immediately generated resentment and a sense of national humiliation. This severe approach fueled the myth that Germany was "stabbed in the back" by its new democratic leaders, critically destabilizing the fragile Weimar Republic. The ensuing international organization, the League of Nations, intended as a system of collective security against aggression, was immediately undermined by the U.S. refusal to join, denying the League the necessary authority and global backing to effectively enforce peace.

The dissolution of the Ottoman Empire (1908–1922) led to the establishment of the League’s Mandate system. While presented under the Wilsonian principle of self-determination and described as a "sacred trust of civilization," this system effectively partitioned the former Ottoman Arab provinces between Britain (Palestine, Iraq) and France (Syria, Lebanon). In practice, the system amounted to a renewed phase of Anglo-French imperial expansion, complicating the region's path to genuine sovereignty.

### **B. Global Economy**

The decade fundamentally restructured the global financial system. Following the outbreak of World War I, most major economic powers immediately suspended the Gold Standard, leading to floating exchange rates and heavily increased inflation across Europe. The U.S. was unique in maintaining the Gold Standard, utilizing the new Federal Reserve to manage gold imports.

The European belligerents financed the war through immense internal borrowing and the liquidation of overseas assets, burning through over $12 billion in foreign assets, representing more than a third of their capital stock built up over the 19th century. By contrast, the long period of U.S. neutrality allowed American industry to mobilize for war production, first by selling goods to Europe and later by massive federal spending upon U.S. entry in 1917\. This created a tremendous economic boom, increasing manufacturing capacity precisely in sectors needed for war, and reducing U.S. unemployment from 7.9% to 1.4%.

This mobilization resulted in a definitive **hegemonic transition**. Wall Street began its ascendancy to global dominance, marking the irreversible decline of the financial hegemony of the City of London. The U.S. transitioned rapidly from a net debtor country to a powerful net creditor by 1919\. This financial shift was so profound that even powerful neutral financial centers like Amsterdam and Zurich benefited, establishing themselves as attractive conduits for post-war reconstruction capital.

By the close of 1920, the economic volatility escalated. Tight monetary consolidation, notably a rise in U.S. interest rates (forcibly followed by central banks worldwide seeking a return to gold parity), triggered a sharp worldwide recession in 1920–1921.

#### **Systemic Fragilities (Economy)**

The post-war economic structure was built upon two critical, interconnected fragilities:

1. **The European Inter-Allied Debt/Reparations Loop:** The U.S. determinedly sought repayment of the more than $10 billion loaned to the Allies, while simultaneously refusing to link these war debts to the German reparations bill. Germany’s mandated reparations were set at a crippling 132 billion gold marks. The requirement for the Allies to repay the U.S. necessitated forcing Germany to pay reparations, but Germany lacked the material capacity to do so, leading to an inherently artificial and unstable cycle of credit dependent almost entirely on U.S. loans and prone to inevitable collapse.  
2. **Fragile Currency Stability in Europe:** The combination of war debt monetization and the threat of foreign occupation over reparations led to spiraling inflation across Europe. In Germany, this pressure culminated in the hyperinflation of the early 1920s. This economic chaos severely undermined the new democratic government, wiping out the savings of the middle class and spurring massive social unrest that gave rise to radical right-wing political forces.

### **C. Social Trends**

The period was defined by mass death and subsequent social re-evaluation. The **1918–1919 Influenza Pandemic** inflicted a staggering demographic shock, killing an estimated 50–100 million people globally, a casualty count significantly higher than the four years of the Great War. Epidemiologically, the virus exhibited an unusual pattern, proving most lethal to young adults aged 20 to 40, thereby compounding the demographic losses already sustained by the war. Studies revealed that mortality rates often correlated inversely with socioeconomic indicators, contradicting the initial belief that the plague was "socially neutral" and instead demonstrating higher mortality rates among poorer populations.

The mass mobilization required by total war accelerated calls for **women's emancipation**. Millions of women filled essential industrial and agricultural roles previously held by men, particularly in war production.13 This contribution provided irresistible political momentum, enabling the realization of nationwide suffrage in the U.S. (19th Amendment, August 1920\) and contributing to victories elsewhere, such as Czechoslovakia (1920).

Conversely, the immediate post-war period saw intense **labor conflict**. The U.S. alone experienced a massive strike wave in 1919, involving over four million workers demanding improved conditions and wages. This struggle for workers' rights coincided exactly with the rise of

**Bolshevik ideology** and the success of the October Revolution. This new political model provided a profound ideological alternative to the capitalist state system. Capital owners and government officials successfully exploited the resulting widespread fear of Bolshevism, labeling anarchists, socialists, and labor activists as foreign agents in the "First Red Scare". This narrative was used to justify the violent suppression of major strikes (such as the steel strike), which proved a crushing defeat for unions, rolling back many gains achieved during the Progressive Era.

### **D. Environmental State**

The environmental record of this decade is dominated by the unprecedented physical destruction caused by industrialized warfare. While formal climate change policy or conservation efforts were minimal compared to today, the industrial scale of WWI led to immense resource consumption, widespread chemical contamination, and the permanent scarring of landscapes along the major fronts. Policy focus remained overwhelmingly fixed on immediate human safety and urban problems. In the U.S., the Public Health Service (formerly the MHS) was officially renamed in 1912 and given expanded responsibilities focusing on controlling contagious diseases, regulating the food supply, and ensuring environmental safety against infectious spread.

### **E. Global Flashpoints**

At the close of 1920, intense friction points promised ongoing instability:

1. **The German Reparations Crisis and Political Instability:** The terms of Versailles created an immediate and potentially explosive situation. The economic burdens, combined with the psychological impact of war guilt, led to the immediate rise of right-wing radicalism and widespread popular belief in the "Stab-in-the-back" myth. A future default on payments by Germany, which was inevitable given the required sum, threatened renewed foreign intervention and political collapse.  
2. **The Polish-Soviet Border Conflict (1919–1921):** The Bolshevik government, through the Comintern (founded 1919), actively pursued the "export of communist revolution" to Europe. This ambition culminated in the Polish-Soviet War, where the Red Army attempted to push the revolution across Poland toward Germany, sparking a critical conflict that defined the boundaries and ideological orientation of Eastern Europe.  
3. **The Turkish War of Independence (1919–1923):** In Anatolia, the Turkish National Movement, led by Mustafa Kemal (Atatürk), actively rebelled against the Allied occupation and the partitioning of the Ottoman Empire stipulated by the Treaty of Sèvres. This rejection of the post-war settlements meant continued violent conflict and state-building, ultimately reshaping the modern borders of the Middle East and resulting in massive population exchanges.  
4. **The New European Successor States' Border Disputes:** The sudden collapse of the multinational empires created a belt of new, ethnically complex states across Central Europe. The latent conflicts over territory, such as the Polish-Lithuanian conflict over regions like Vilnius, were now manifesting as diplomatic and military disputes, threatening the new, precarious borders drawn by the victorious powers.

### **F. Emerging Paradigms**

1. **General Relativity and the New Physics:** The definitive verification of Albert Einstein's General Theory of Relativity in November 1919, based on observations of the light bending around the Sun during the May 29 solar eclipse, marked a scientific watershed. This monumental achievement fundamentally superseded Isaac Newton's mechanics and redefined humanity’s understanding of space, time, and gravity. The intellectual revolution opened new fields of inquiry into modern cosmology, including the theoretical basis for black holes and gravitational waves.  
2. **Commercial Radio Broadcasting:** The introduction of commercial audio radio broadcasting, exemplified by KDKA in Pittsburgh in November 1920, harnessed wartime advancements in vacuum tube technology. This was a pivotal moment, shifting wireless communication from military/telegraphic use (Morse code) to the transmission of news and entertainment, creating the world’s first electronic mass communications medium. While nascent in 1920, this technology had the potential for massive, disruptive cultural and political growth, promising to unify culture and centralize messaging in the following decade.  
3. **The Comintern and Global Revolutionary Organization:** The foundation of the Communist International (Comintern) in Moscow in March 1919 represented a significant new paradigm in transnational political action. It centralized global communist parties under the direction of the Bolsheviks, creating a disciplined, cohesive organization focused explicitly on the transnational overthrow of existing capitalist states. This model posed an organizational challenge far greater than previous decentralized socialist movements.

### **G. Systemic Fragilities (1920)**

The analysis reveals three critical vulnerabilities inherent in the new post-1920 global system:

1. **The Inter-Allied Debt Structure:** The refusal by the U.S. to mitigate war debts by linking them to Germany's reparations obligations ensured that European financial recovery was predicated on a fictional credit loop. This created a debt structure incapable of independent operation, guaranteeing a future economic crisis once American loans dried up or Germany defaulted on its obligations.  
2. **Public Health Resilience:** Despite the unparalleled mortality of the 1918 pandemic, which far exceeded wartime destruction, the global political and social systems failed to establish sustained preparedness. Policy interventions (Non-Pharmaceutical Interventions, or NPIs) were often implemented late, maintained for short durations (less than six weeks in many U.S. cities), and met with significant pushback, demonstrating a persistent political and social vulnerability to high-mortality, rapidly spreading pathogens.  
3. **The Fragile Democracies of Central Europe:** The newly formed republics, such as the Weimar Republic and the successor states of the collapsed empires, lacked the institutional depth and political legitimacy necessary to withstand the instantaneous and immense pressures placed upon them. They were simultaneously subjected to foreign debt demands, internal border conflicts, and radical ideological challenges. The democratic institutions were surprisingly vulnerable to internal and external revisionism, particularly given the widespread popular discontent over the peace settlement terms.

The geopolitical pivot of 1911–1920 shifted global power dynamics away from the continental European center, placing new and profound responsibility on the United States and creating an ideological counterweight in Bolshevik Russia.

### **2\. The British Empire**

**Domestic Politics:** The war mobilization saw British society undergo immense stress, though the strength of the City minimized the inflationary effects seen elsewhere. Trade union membership swelled during the war, peaking at 8.3 million in 1920\. The most significant domestic challenge was the rising tide of militant republican separatism in Ireland, which by 1919 had eclipsed the moderate Home Rule movement, leading to the outbreak of the Irish War of Independence.

**Foreign Policy and International Relations:** Britain, led by Prime Minister David Lloyd George, was one of the "Big Four" that dominated the Paris Peace Conference. The Empire successfully secured Mandates over key territories in the Middle East—Palestine, Transjordan, and Iraq—effectively expanding its imperial domain under the guise of League of Nations supervision. Diplomacy was focused on managing the immense costs of war and securing debt repayment, as formalized in the Balfour Note of 1922\.

**Economic Performance:** While Britain suffered massive financial attrition, losing significant foreign assets 10, it retained the largest overall foreign capital stock of any country immediately after the war. However, the financial system struggled to cope with demobilization, rising labor costs, and the sudden shift in global monetary power towards the U.S..10 Consumer consumption fell 18% from 1914 to 1919\.

**Key Individuals:** David Lloyd George (Prime Minister, influential negotiator at Versailles), Lord Balfour (Foreign Secretary, associated with Middle East policies).

**Key Organizations:** The City of London (battling to maintain global financial centrality), the Irish Parliamentary Party (IPP, losing influence to republicans).

### **3\. Bolshevik Russia (USSR Inception)**

**Domestic Politics:** The Bolshevik faction, led by Vladimir Lenin, seized power in October 1917, establishing a rigidly centralized political regime aimed at establishing the "dictatorship of the proletariat". The period 1918–1921 was consumed by the brutal Russian Civil War against the anti-Bolshevik White forces and foreign interventionists. The Bolsheviks secured victory by 1920 due to their superior military strategy (under Leon Trotsky), unified ideology, and control of industrial resources, but the conflict resulted in approximately 7 million deaths.

**Foreign Policy and International Relations:** Revolutionary success immediately translated into aggressive international outreach through the founding of the **Comintern (Communist International)** in 1919\. This organization was designed to coordinate global communist parties and force them to break from traditional socialists, thereby prioritizing world revolution. The direct military test of this policy came during the Polish-Soviet War of 1920, where the Red Army’s attempt to export the revolution westward was decisively repulsed by Polish forces at Warsaw. Following this failure, the Soviet government adopted a dual-track foreign policy: engaging in formal diplomatic relations with capitalist states while simultaneously working through the Comintern to foment revolution.

**Economic Performance:** During this period, the economy operated under "War Communism," characterized by extreme centralization, state control, and severe economic deprivation necessary to sustain the military machine. The focus on political and military victory resulted in widespread industrial collapse and massive social hardship.

**Key Individuals:** Vladimir I. Lenin (leader and primary theorist of Bolshevism), Leon Trotsky (military organizer of the Red Army).

**Key Organizations:** The Bolshevik Party (Communist Party), the Comintern (Third International).

### **4\. Weimar Germany**

**Domestic Politics:** The collapse of the German Empire in 1918 led to the establishment of the democratic Weimar Republic. However, the government was destabilized from its inception by economic hardship, political fragmentation, and the deep national humiliation resulting from the Versailles Treaty. Many Germans internalized the "Stab-in-the-back" myth, believing the democratic leadership had surrendered German honor, paving the way for the rise of radical right-wing parties seeking to regain prestige through remilitarization.

**Foreign Policy and International Relations:** Germany’s foreign policy was wholly dictated by the terms of the peace settlement. It was stripped of substantial territories, had its colonies confiscated, and saw its military strength crippled. The ongoing requirement to pay reparations became the single greatest point of friction with the Allied powers.

**Economic Performance:** Despite inherent structural strengths, the German economy struggled severely due to war losses, diminished industrial capacity (partially caused by reduced productivity from poor nutrition and replacement labor), and the initial imposition of reparations. The massive war debt monetization set the stage for the spiraling hyperinflation that would dominate the economy immediately after 1920\.

## **IV. Thematic Sections**

### **A. Science and Technology**

The decade saw a fundamental reordering of physics and the militarization of new technologies.

The verification of **Albert Einstein's General Theory of Relativity** proved to be the decade’s most profound scientific achievement. Published in its final form in 1915, the theory proposed that gravity was a distortion of spacetime itself. The crucial empirical proof came in November 1919, when Arthur Eddington’s expeditions confirmed that starlight was indeed deflected by the Sun's gravitational field exactly as Einstein predicted. This event simultaneously overthrew the 200-year dominance of Newtonian gravity and catapulted Einstein to global fame. The theoretical framework immediately established the foundation for modern cosmology and predicted extreme astronomical phenomena such as black holes and gravitational waves.

World War I acted as an immense accelerator for applied technology:

* **Aviation:** Aircraft technology evolved with unprecedented speed, moving from initial limited reconnaissance roles to establishing the core air power doctrines of intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance, and strike capability by 1918\. This wartime refinement directly enabled the subsequent emergence of commercial aviation and air transport infrastructure.  
* **Chemical Warfare:** The war saw the highly organized application of science to mass destruction, notably in the German chemical warfare program, establishing a problematic precedent for the future engagement of scientific talent in developing sophisticated weaponry.  
* **Communications:** The perfection of vacuum tube technology during the war led directly to the successful transmission of high-quality audio signals, culminating in the establishment of the first commercial radio broadcasts in 1920\.

### **B. Global Health**

The single most defining global health event was the **1918–1919 Influenza Pandemic**, commonly known as the Spanish Flu (though its origins are traced potentially to the U.S.). The pandemic infected approximately a fifth of the world's population, killing somewhere between 50 and 100 million people, a staggering toll that dwarfed the military fatalities of the Great War. Its unusual lethality for young adults (ages 20–40) made it particularly devastating to the workforce and military demographics.

In the realm of medical science, necessity drove significant advancements:

* **Blood Transfusion:** Previously difficult and often fatal due to clotting and incompatibility, transfusion became a widely accepted battlefield medical treatment during the war. Innovations included the practical application of ABO blood grouping (discovered in 1900\) for safe matching, and the development of the "citrate method," which prevented blood clotting and allowed for the storage and transport of universal donor whole blood to forward medical units. This was regarded by the Royal Army Medical Corps as the war's most crucial medical advance.  
* **Public Health Policy:** Institutional capacity increased with the renaming and expansion of the U.S. Public Health Service in 1912, granting it greater responsibility for controlling contagious diseases and biomedical research. Despite the severity of the pandemic, attempts to implement Non-Pharmaceutical Interventions (NPIs) like mask ordinances, bans on public gatherings, and staggered business hours were often temporary and met with defiance and political pushback, demonstrating a historical challenge to enforcing public health measures in democratic societies.

### **C. Security and Conflict**

The security environment of the decade was fundamentally transformed by the practice of **total war**, where entire national populations and industrial bases were mobilized for conflict. The resulting slaughter led to a widespread international movement to prevent future wars, culminating in the League of Nations proposal.

However, the period immediately following the 1918 Armistice was characterized by intense, localized conflicts stemming from imperial collapse and ideological struggle:

* **The Russian Civil War** was the central conflict of the East, defining the Bolshevik state’s power and resulting in massive loss of life.  
* The **Polish-Soviet War** represented the direct ideological and military clash between Bolshevik revolutionary expansionism and the newly established democratic states of Central Europe.  
* The **Turkish War of Independence** was a nationalist military campaign that rejected the terms of partition imposed by the Allies, ensuring continued conflict in Anatolia and the Middle East until new, recognized borders could be established.

Diplomatic efforts centered on the Paris Peace Conference were largely ineffective in securing long-term peace. The Treaty of Versailles was designed to punish and isolate Germany, not integrate it into a new political order. This diplomatic failure to create a mutually acceptable peace, combined with the U.S. failure to join the League of Nations, ensured that the mechanisms intended for conflict resolution were incapable of addressing the systemic pressures and revisionist goals that defined the post-war world.

### **D. Culture and Society**

The sheer trauma and scale of destruction in the Great War provoked a profound cultural rejection of established bourgeois and nationalistic values.

**Artistic Movements:** This intellectual disillusionment gave rise to **Dadaism**, which began in neutral Zurich in 1916\. Dada was a revolutionary "anti-art" movement that deliberately favored satire, spontaneity, chance, and nonsense as a direct and vehement reaction against the stale cultural traditions that its proponents believed had led to the war. Heavily influenced by Cubism and Futurism, Dada spread internationally to New York, Berlin, and Paris, becoming the immediate antecedent to Surrealism and laying the intellectual foundation for Conceptual Art.

**Societal Values and Norms:** The most tangible shift in social values centered on the role of women. The expansion of women into traditional male industrial and military support roles during the war created leverage for political change. Advocates successfully argued that women's inherent qualities, such as morality and "social motherhood," would benefit the state if they were admitted to political life, effectively using essentialist ideology to counter opposition to political equality. This culminated in sweeping suffrage victories across the Western world. Meanwhile, society became intensely polarized between the forces of organized labor and radical politics (Bolshevism) and the forces of political and industrial conservatism, which utilized anti-communist sentiment to violently suppress social reform movements.

## **V. Conclusion**

The period 1911–1920 represents a cataclysmic acceleration of global history, defined by the definitive **Systemic Rupture** of the 19th-century order. It was an era where military, demographic, economic, and intellectual crises converged, ensuring that the trajectory of the 20th century would be one of instability and ideological confrontation.

The analysis confirms three simultaneous, non-linear transformations:

1. **Geopolitical Deconsolidation:** The rapid collapse of four empires and the failure of the Versailles architects to establish a sustainable peace replaced imperial stability with a multitude of fragile, contentious nation-states. The creation of the Mandate system merely masked renewed Anglo-French imperial control in strategic areas. The failure of the U.S. to support the League of Nations ensured that the new global security framework was stillborn.  
2. **Financial Revolution:** The war immediately and fundamentally transferred global financial power. The rise of the United States as the world's creditor marked the definitive end of European financial hegemony. This shift simultaneously created the most critical systemic fragility: a global economy built upon an unsustainable loop of inter-Allied debts and impossible German reparations.  
3. **Ideological Confrontation:** The establishment and consolidation of Bolshevik power, formalized through the Comintern, introduced a centralized, aggressively exported ideological challenge to the liberal capitalist order. This ideological schism, cemented by the Polish-Soviet War, replaced the old imperial competition with a new, enduring conflict between Communist and Liberal systems.

The world concluded 1920 exhausted and highly unstable. The immense trauma of the war was compounded by the demographic devastation of the pandemic. Instead of ushering in an era of peace and democracy, the decade planted the seeds of further conflict. The unresolved economic grievances laid the foundation for global financial collapse, while the political resentment fostered by the Treaty of Versailles—the "Stab-in-the-back" myth—provided the critical ideological feedstock for the radical right-wing movements that would drive the world toward a second, larger conflagration. The snapshot of 1920 shows a world suspended between the total destruction of the old order and the terrifying uncertainty of the new.
